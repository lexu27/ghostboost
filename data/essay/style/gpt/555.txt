Throughout literary history, the ethical quandaries posed by scientific progress have been a recurring theme, explored by numerous esteemed writers. The works of Robert Louis Stevenson's "The Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde" and Mary Shelley's "Frankenstein" delve deeply into the shadowy realms of scientific inquiry. This essay delves into how both narratives convey the idea that science can serve as a double-edged sword, capable of both violence and moral decay. By scrutinizing the actions and intentions of the scientists within each tale and examining the repercussions of their experiments, we unearth a profound realization regarding the hazards of unbridled scientific advancement.
