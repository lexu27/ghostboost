The 17th century witnessed a significant era of colonial expansion in the Americas, ushering in profound alterations in the statuses and circumstances of native and African women. The intrusion and formation of European colonial communities had a widespread influence on the societal and economic structures of indigenous tribes. Native women underwent changes concerning work, commerce, and the adoption of Christianity, while African women assumed diverse roles within the Fetu and Bakongo tribes. These roles were molded by social rankings and traditions, with their independence and entitlements gravely affected by their enslaved conditions. Delving into the narrative of Queen Njinga offers deeper insights into the opposition to Portuguese colonization in Kongo.
