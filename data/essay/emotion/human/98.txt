Beowulf is often considered one of the most prominent ancient European poems, with its origin sparking various debates. Described as "a Danish poem in the Anglo-Saxon dialect," the narrative showcases the heroic deeds of Beowulf, a figure believed to be divinely sent to aid the people. While assisting King Hrothgar by defeating the monster Grendel and his mother, Beowulf later faces a dragon threatening his kingdom. Despite triumphing over the beast, he meets his demise from fatal wounds. The poem delves into significant aspects of human life, such as the importance of ancestry in shaping identity and the transition to maturity and responsibility with age. It also explores the conflict between individual values and societal norms, presenting a nuanced portrayal of human nature amidst conflicting belief systems.
